# Adult Head-to-Toe Examination
**Source:** Sherpath — Elsevier

---

## Overview

Performing an adult head-to-toe examination requires integration of history taking, assessment skills, and communication with the patient.

**Learning Objective:** Describe the process of conducting the adult head-to-toe examination.

**Lesson Sections:**
1. Adult Head-to-Toe Examination: Patient Seated
2. Adult Head-to-Toe Examination: Patient Lying
3. Adult Head-to-Toe Examination: Patient Standing
4. Adult Functional Assessment

---

## Section 1: Adult Head-to-Toe Examination — Patient Seated

### Pre-Examination: General Inspection Considerations

The physical examination begins with the initial patient encounter. To prepare for successful assessment:

- Review the patient's health history — medical, surgical, and family history (history and physical exam are interrelated)
- Ask the patient about their chief complaint; think critically about causes; look for related signs and symptoms throughout
- Gather appropriate equipment (stethoscope, vital sign equipment, penlight, gloves, etc.)
- Ensure privacy and comfort (close the door, pull the curtain, adjust room temperature)
- Record both normal and abnormal findings throughout

---

### Seated Examination Sequence

The examination should flow smoothly, minimize the number of times the patient changes positions, and conserve energy. Modify as needed per patient and situation.

#### Step 1 — General Inspection *(begins at initial greeting)*
Observe for:
- Skin color
- Facial expression
- Mobility
- Dress and posture
- Speech and hearing
- Orientation and mental alertness

#### Step 2 — Preparation
Respecting the patient's privacy, instruct the patient to:
- Empty the bladder
- Remove as much clothing as necessary and put on a gown
- Request a chaperone if necessary (consider patient's age, gender, and type of exam — pelvic and rectal)

#### Step 3 — Measurements and Vital Signs
Document:
- Temperature
- Pulse
- Respiration
- Blood pressure
- Oxygen saturation

---

### Patient Seated: Wearing Gown

Assessments while the patient is seated wearing a gown cover the head and face, eyes, ears, nose, mouth and pharynx, neck, and upper extremities.

#### Step 4 — Head & Face
- Inspect skin characteristics
- Inspect and palpate scalp and hair for texture, distribution, and quantity
- Palpate facial bones and temporomandibular joint (TMJ) while patient opens and closes mouth
- Inspect patient's ability to clench jaw, squeeze eyes shut, wrinkle forehead, smile, and puff out cheeks (CNs V and VII)

#### Step 5 — Eyes
- Examine eyelids, eyelashes, and palpebral folds
- Inspect the iris, sclera, and conjunctiva
- Test pupillary response to light and accommodation
- Test extraocular movements (CNs III, IV, and VI) and assess visual fields (CN II)
- Perform ophthalmic examination for red reflex; inspect lens, disc, cup margins, vessels, and retinal surfaces

#### Step 6 — Ears
- Inspect alignment and placement
- Inspect surface characteristics
- Palpate auricle
- Screen hearing with whisper test (CN VIII)
- Perform Rinne and Weber tests, if indicated

#### Step 7 — Nose
- Note structure and position of septum
- Determine patency of each nostril
- Inspect mucosa, septum, and turbinates

#### Step 8 — Mouth & Pharynx
- Inspect lips, gums, and mouth for color, surface characteristics, and any apparent abnormalities
- Inspect oropharynx
- Inspect teeth for color, number, and surface characteristics
- Inspect tongue for color, characteristics, symmetry, and movement (CN XII)
- Test gag reflex and soft palate rising by asking patient to say "ah" (CNs IX and X)

#### Step 9 — Neck
- Inspect neck and thyroid for symmetry and smoothness
- Inspect and palpate range of motion; test resistance against examiner's hand
- Test shoulder shrug (CN XI)
- Palpate carotid pulses, one at a time (also when patient is supine)
- Palpate tracheal position
- Palpate thyroid
- Palpate lymph nodes
- Auscultate carotid arteries and thyroid

#### Step 10 — Upper Extremities
- Inspect skin and nails
- Inspect symmetry of muscle mass
- Inspect and palpate hands, arms, and shoulders
- Assess joint range of motion and muscle strength: fingers, wrists, elbows, and shoulders
- Assess pulses: radial, brachial

---

### Patient Seated: Back and Chest Exposed

*For males: gown pulled down to expose entire chest and back. For females: expose back while leaving breasts covered until ready to examine anterior chest.*

#### Step 11 — Back & Posterior Chest
- Inspect skin and thorax
- Inspect symmetry of the shoulders and musculoskeletal development
- Inspect and palpate the scapula and spine
- Palpate and percuss costovertebral angle, if indicated

#### Step 12 — Lungs (Posterior)
- Observe respirations for depth, rhythm, and pattern
- Palpate for expansion and tactile fremitus
- Palpate scapular and subscapular nodes
- Percuss posterior chest and lateral walls systematically for resonance
- Percuss to measure diaphragmatic excursion
- Auscultate systematically for breath sounds; note characteristics and adventitious sounds

#### Step 13 — Anterior Chest, Lungs, and Heart
- Inspect skin, musculoskeletal development, and symmetry
- Assess respirations, patient posture, and respiratory effort
- Palpate chest wall for crepitation and tenderness
- Palpate precordium for thrills, heaves, and pulsations
- Palpate left side of chest to locate apical pulse
- Palpate for tactile fremitus
- Palpate axillary lymph nodes
- Percuss systematically for resonance
- Auscultate systematically for breath sounds
- Auscultate systematically for heart sounds: aortic, pulmonic, second pulmonic, mitral, and tricuspid areas

#### Step 14 — Breasts
**Female:**
- Inspect breasts in four positions: arms hanging loosely at sides, extended overhead or flexed behind neck, pushing hands on hips, and leaning forward from the waist
- Perform bimanual digital palpation of breast tissue

**Male:**
- Inspect breasts and nipples for symmetry, enlargement, and surface characteristics
- Palpate breast tissue

## Section 2: Adult Head-to-Toe Examination — Patient Lying

### Patient Reclining at 45 Degrees

#### Step 15 — Jugular Assessment
- Assist patient to a reclining position at a 45-degree angle; stand to the side
- Inspect the patient's chest while reclining
- Assess jugular venous distention and jugular pulsations
  - *Jugular pulse:* a pulsation in the jugular vein caused by conditions that inhibit diastolic filling of the right side of the heart

---

### Patient Supine: Chest and Abdomen Exposed

*Assist patient to supine position. If unable to lie flat, maintain a 30-degree angle. Uncover chest and abdomen while keeping lower extremities draped. For females, consider covering the chest before moving to abdominal assessment. Follow head-to-toe order; inspect abdomen before percussing it.*

#### Step 16 — Female Breast (Supine)
- Palpate all areas of breast tissue systematically using light, medium, and deep palpation with patient's arm over her head
- Depress nipple into the areola

#### Step 17 — Heart (Supine)
- Palpate chest wall for thrills, heaves, and pulsations
- Auscultate systematically; may turn patient slightly to the left side and repeat auscultation

#### Step 18 — Abdomen
- Inspect skin characteristics, contour, pulsations, and movement
- Auscultate all quadrants for bowel sounds
- Auscultate the aorta and renal, iliac, and femoral arteries for bruits
- Percuss all quadrants for tone
- Percuss liver borders and estimate span
- Lightly palpate all quadrants, then deeply palpate all quadrants
- Palpate midline for aortic pulsation
- Abdominal reflexes may be tested (optional)
- Ask patient to raise head to inspect abdominal muscles

#### Step 19 — Inguinal Area
- Palpate for lymph nodes, pulses, and hernias

#### Step 20 — External Genitalia (Males, Supine)
- Inspect penis, urethral meatus, scrotum, and pubic hair
- Palpate scrotal contents
- Test the cremasteric reflex

---

### Patient Supine: Legs Exposed

*After completing chest and abdomen assessment, rearrange drapes to cover the abdomen and expose the lower extremities.*

#### Step 21 — Hips
- Palpate hips for stability
- Test range of motion and strength of hips

#### Step 22 — Legs / Thighs
- Inspect for skin characteristics, hair distribution, and muscle mass

#### Step 23 — Feet, Ankles & Knees
- Palpate for temperature, texture, edema, and pulses (dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial, popliteal)
- Test range of motion and strength of toes, feet, ankles, and knees

---

### Adult Female Patient — Lithotomy Position

*For female patients requiring genitalia examination. Don gloves and assist the patient into the lithotomy position. Drape appropriately to maintain privacy; place a stool at the foot of the examining table if needed.*

#### Step 24 — External Genitalia (Females)
- Examine external genitalia: pubic hair, labia, perineum, and anus
- Palpate the labia and Bartholin glands

#### Step 25 — Speculum Examination
- Perform speculum examination to inspect the vagina and cervix
- Collect specimens if needed

#### Step 26 — Bimanual Palpation
- Stand and perform bimanual palpation to assess the uterus and cervix

#### Step 27 — Rectal Examination (Female)
- Perform a rectovaginal or rectal examination if indicated

---

## Section 3: Adult Head-to-Toe Examination — Patient Standing

*Assist patient to standing position. Stand next to patient for observation. Maintain patient safety — stay within arm's reach to assist back to seated position if necessary.*

#### Step 28 — Spine
- Ask patient to bend over at the waist
- Inspect and palpate the spine
- Observe range of motion during: flexion, hyperextension, lateral bending, and rotation of the trunk

#### Step 29 — Neurologic Assessment (Standing)
- Observe gait, balance, and proprioception
- Perform the **Romberg test** *(patient stands with feet together, eyes closed — assess balance)*
- Ask the patient to walk **heel-to-toe**
- Ask the patient to **stand on one foot, then the other**, with eyes closed
- Ask the patient to **hop in place** on one foot, then the other
- Assess for inguinal and femoral hernias

#### Step 30 — External Genitalia (Males, Standing)
- Inspect the glans penis, urethral opening, and scrotum
- Palpate the scrotum for testes and presence of a hernia or hydrocele

#### Step 31 — Rectal Examination (Males)
- Ask patient to lean over the examination table with arms and chest resting on the table
- Have patient point toes inward to relax the buttocks
- Inspect the sacrococcygeal and perianal areas
- Perform rectal examination: palpate sphincter tone and circumferentially for rectal masses
- Palpate the prostate gland
- Note characteristics of stool if found on gloved finger removal
- Test for occult blood if indicated

## Section 4: Adult Functional Assessment

### Overview

A functional assessment is essential for older adult patients and appropriate for any patient limited by disease, injury, or disability.

**Purpose:** Determine the extent to which Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) can be accomplished, including self-care activities (bathing, toileting) and other activities (driving, preparing meals).

**Components of the Functional Examination:**
1. Comprehensive history
2. Physical examination
3. Social assessment
4. Detailed list of all medications (patient-provided)

---

### Activities of Daily Living

*Also assess use of assistive devices for ambulation and function (e.g., cane, walker, commode chair, hospital bed).*

**Basic ADLs:**
- Bathing
- Dressing
- Toileting
- Ambulation
- Feeding

**Instrumental ADLs:**
- Housekeeping
- Grocery shopping
- Meal preparation
- Medication management
- Communication skills
- Money management

---

### System Review and Social Assessment

**System Review** — Common problems to assess in older adults:
- Nutritional deficiencies and malnutrition
- Urinary incontinence
- Memory changes and signs of dementia
- Depression
- Medication-induced delirium
- Prior falls or fear of falling

**Social Assessment:**
- Identify current and probable future caregivers
- Assess caregivers' abilities and resources
- Assess financial resources and health insurance
- Ask about advance directives or a durable power of attorney for health care decisions

---

### Physical Assessment

The physical examination component of the functional assessment is focused on common problems of older adults:

- **Mental status** — assess cognition, memory, and mood
- **Respiratory** — evaluate for dyspnea on exertion
- **Cardiovascular** — measure blood pressure in both sitting and standing positions to assess for orthostatic hypotension
- **Musculoskeletal** — evaluate strength
- **Neurologic** — pay special attention to balance, coordination, and gait
- **Skin** — inspect for signs of decubitus or venous stasis ulcers

---

## Summary

The adult head-to-toe examination should be performed quickly and accurately, with the goals of maintaining patient comfort and obtaining vital information.

- Some components are best performed **seated**, others while **lying** or **standing**
- Structure the examination based on the **patient's needs and situation**, minimizing position changes
- Perform a **functional examination when indicated** to assess ability to perform basic and instrumental ADLs
